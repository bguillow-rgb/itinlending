How to Get a Personal Loan With an ITIN Number (2026)

By ITIN Lending Editorial Team · Published June 8, 2026

Quick Answer

Yes — you can get a personal loan with an ITIN number and no SSN. Credit unions, CDFIs, and select fintech lenders all accept ITINs. Expect loan amounts from $1,000 to $35,000+, rates typically higher than SSN-based loans, and approval based on income, repayment history, and credit profile.

Getting a personal loan without a Social Security number feels like hitting a wall — you know you can afford the payment, you have income, and then the application stops cold the moment it asks for an SSN. Here is what actually happens next, and how to move forward.

Can I really qualify for a personal loan using just my ITIN?

A question we hear often: is the ITIN actually accepted, or is it just a rumor?

From a legal standpoint, no federal law prohibits lenders from issuing personal loans to borrowers without Social Security numbers. Lenders need a way to identify borrowers and verify tax reporting obligations, and an ITIN satisfies that requirement. Whether a lender accepts an ITIN is a business decision, not a legal limitation.

Borrowers without a Social Security number can still qualify for a personal loan using an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number. These personal loans with ITIN may be unsecured or secured and typically require verifiable income, a credit score or acceptable alternative credit history, and carry higher interest rates than standard personal loans.

The catch is finding the right lender. Most major national banks do not accept ITINs for personal loans. Credit unions, online lenders, and specialty finance companies that offer programs specifically for ITIN borrowers are more realistic options.

What kinds of lenders actually accept ITINs in 2026?

A few categories of lenders work with ITIN holders. Many local and regional credit unions accept ITINs, especially in states with large immigrant populations. Examples include Self-Help Federal Credit Union, Latino Community Credit Union, and Mission Asset Fund. Membership is usually based on geography or community ties.

Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs) are nonprofit lenders certified by the U.S. Treasury that focus on underbanked communities. They typically accept ITINs and have lower interest rates than online lenders. These are often the best-kept secret for ITIN borrowers — worth searching the CDFI Fund locator at cdfi.cdfifund.gov before turning to any online marketplace.

Online fintech lenders that explicitly accept ITINs include MoneyLion, which accepts ITINs for some products through its credit-builder loan, and EzLoan, which markets to thin-file and ITIN borrowers with personal loans up to $5,000. Camino Financial and Stilt are two additional names that come up frequently in this space for immigrant borrowers.

For borrowers who already have a banking relationship, it is worth asking directly. According to WalletHub, Wells Fargo offers competitive personal loans that can be applied for with an ITIN if you don’t have an SSN, with one of the lower APR ranges available — but to be eligible, you need to have a Wells Fargo account open for at least 12 months. PNC may loan $1,000–$35,000 with an APR as low as 8.14%, depending on overall creditworthiness.

What interest rates and loan amounts should I expect?

This one comes up a lot. Rates for ITIN personal loans are real — but they are not always punishing if you shop carefully.

Most major banks require a Social Security number and do not consider ITIN applications. Lenders that do accept ITINs often charge higher ITIN loan interest rates, even when the borrower’s income and repayment profile are otherwise strong. ITIN loan lenders view these loans as higher risk for several reasons — the borrower pool is smaller and less studied, meaning lenders have less data to inform their underwriting decisions.

The practical rate difference matters. Loan amounts typically run $1,000 to $15,000, with most ITIN lenders capping at $5,000–$10,000. A $5,000 loan at 24% APR over 36 months carries monthly payments around $196 and total interest of about $2,068 — whereas a credit union or CDFI at 14% APR would cut that to about $171 per month and $1,156 in total interest. That $912 difference is a strong reason to apply to a credit union first.

Online and specialty ITIN loan lenders often charge higher interest rates and fees than credit unions or community banks. Speed and accessibility are prioritized, which makes careful review of total loan cost essential before accepting an offer.

What documents do I need to apply?

Typically you will need your IRS ITIN assignment letter, passport, state-issued ID or driver’s license, recent pay stubs or bank statements, proof of address, and any visa documents if applicable.

One critical detail many people miss: a valid ITIN is the baseline requirement — your Individual Taxpayer Identification Number must be current. The IRS began expiring ITINs in 2017 for numbers that were not used on a tax return for three consecutive years. If your ITIN has expired, it will usually need to be renewed before a lender can move forward with your application.

Verify your ITIN status before you apply for anything. You can check by calling the IRS at 1-800-829-1040 or by reviewing whether your most recent tax return was accepted with that number.

How does the lender decide whether to approve me?

Readers frequently ask whether immigration status is a factor — and the answer is largely no.

ITIN eligibility and immigration status are separate considerations. ITINs are issued to a range of individuals, including certain visa holders, non-resident aliens with U.S. income, and dependents who are required to file U.S. tax returns but do not qualify for Social Security numbers. Lenders assess income stability, credit history, and repayment capacity rather than visa category.

Credit score requirements vary widely. Some ITIN loan programs require traditional credit scores, often starting around 580. Others rely on alternative credit documentation, such as rental payment history or utility bills. If you have no U.S. credit file at all, the ITIN personal loan path is harder but not closed — CDFIs and some credit unions will manually review 12 months of alternative payment history.

Interest rates are determined by your credit profile (if one exists), income, and debt-to-income ratio. Because many ITIN holders are also building their credit history from scratch, some lenders may view these loans as higher risk — which can result in higher interest rates compared to standard loans, but they remain a far safer and more affordable alternative to predatory high-interest lenders.

For borrowers on an employment-based visa, approval odds also improve based on visa type. For visa holders, your visa type affects approval — H-1B, L-1, and other employment-based visas are easier to get approved for than tourist or short-term visas.

Will the loan actually build my U.S. credit score?

Yes — if you pick the right lender. When the lender reports the loan to the major credit bureaus, credit bureaus track your credit file under your ITIN the same way they track an SSN holder’s file. Make sure to ask the lender if they report.

This matters more than most borrowers realize. An ITIN personal loan that reports to all three bureaus is simultaneously a borrowing tool and a credit-building tool — something especially valuable if you eventually want a mortgage or an ITIN car loan at better rates down the road.

If you have time, build U.S. credit before you apply for a personal loan. Even three to six months of on-time payments on a credit-builder card give you a thin but workable file that dramatically improves your loan options. If you are not in a rush, this is the highest-return step you can take.

What are the biggest mistakes ITIN borrowers make when applying?

This one comes up a lot, especially from readers who were denied the first time around.

Applying at the wrong lender first. Walking into a major national bank with just an ITIN is almost always a dead end. Applications often break down not because the borrower is unqualified, but because lender criteria and pricing structures differ sharply. Start with credit unions and CDFIs in your area, then move to fintech lenders if needed.

Letting an expired ITIN derail you. The IRS began expiring unused ITINs in 2017. If you filed taxes at least once every three years, you are likely fine — but confirm before you apply, not after a lender declines you.

Ignoring total cost. A lower advertised rate with high origination fees can cost more than a slightly higher rate with no fees. Always compare the APR — the annual percentage rate — not just the interest rate number. That is especially important when comparing online lenders, which sometimes bury their fees.

Missing a co-signer opportunity. If possible, having a U.S. citizen or permanent resident co-sign your loan can improve your chances of approval and potentially secure better terms. Not every lender allows co-signers, but for those that do, it can be the difference between approval and denial when your U.S. credit file is thin.

Falling for predatory lenders. Some lenders may take advantage of ITIN holders’ limited options. Research lenders thoroughly, read all terms carefully, and be wary of offers that seem too good to be true. Any offer with triple-digit APRs or mandatory insurance add-ons should be declined immediately.

Step-by-step: how to apply for an ITIN personal loan

  1. Confirm your ITIN is active. Check that you have filed at least one U.S. tax return in the past three years. If your ITIN has expired, file IRS Form W-7 to renew it before applying anywhere.
  2. Pull your free credit report. Visit AnnualCreditReport.com and check what (if any) U.S. credit history exists under your ITIN. Knowing your starting point shapes which lenders to target.
  3. Apply at a credit union or CDFI first. Their rates are best, and they’re more accommodating to ITIN applicants. Search for immigrant-serving credit unions in your state or use the CDFI Fund locator.
  4. Gather your documents in advance. ITIN assignment letter, photo ID, two to three months of pay stubs or bank statements, proof of address. Having these ready shortens approval time significantly.
  5. If declined locally, try ITIN-focused fintechs. If declined, try a fintech lender that explicitly serves ITIN holders (Stilt, Camino Financial, EzLoan). Use a marketplace like LendingTree to compare offers without multiple hard pulls.
  6. Compare APRs, not just rates. Get at least two or three quotes. The difference between a 14% and a 24% APR on a $5,000 loan is nearly $1,000 in total interest over three years — that is real money worth 30 minutes of comparison shopping.
  7. Ask whether the lender reports to all three bureaus. If building U.S. credit is part of your goal, this question is non-negotiable.

Common questions

Can I get a personal loan with only an ITIN and no Social Security number?

Yes. Several lenders — including credit unions, Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs), and fintech lenders — accept an ITIN in place of an SSN. No federal law prohibits issuing personal loans to borrowers without an SSN; accepting an ITIN is simply a business decision each lender makes.

What credit score do I need for an ITIN personal loan?

Requirements vary. Many lenders start around a 580–620 FICO score, while the best overall loans from institutions like WalletHub's top picks typically require 660+. If you have no U.S. credit history at all, some lenders accept alternative credit documentation such as 12 months of on-time rent or utility payments.

Will an ITIN personal loan help build my U.S. credit score?

It can — but only if the lender reports to the major credit bureaus (Experian, TransUnion, and Equifax). Credit bureaus track your file under your ITIN the same way they track an SSN holder's file. Always ask a lender whether they report before you sign.

How much can I borrow with an ITIN personal loan?

Most ITIN-accepting lenders cap loans at $5,000–$15,000, though some credit unions go higher. For example, Wells Fargo accepts ITINs for existing customers and offers larger amounts, and PNC may lend up to $35,000 to qualified borrowers with an ITIN.

What documents do I need to apply for a personal loan with an ITIN?

Standard documents include: your IRS ITIN assignment letter (CP565), a valid government-issued photo ID (passport or state ID), recent pay stubs or bank statements showing income, proof of your U.S. address, and any applicable visa documents. Make sure your ITIN is current — the IRS began expiring unused ITINs in 2017.

Are ITIN personal loan interest rates higher than regular loans?

Generally yes. Lenders that accept ITINs often charge higher rates than standard personal loans because they have less borrower data to underwrite, credit reporting can be inconsistent, and the borrower pool is smaller. Rates at credit unions and CDFIs are typically far lower than at online specialty lenders.

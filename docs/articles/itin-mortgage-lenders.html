Best ITIN Mortgage Lenders: How to Find One (2026)

By Editorial Staff · Research & Fact-Checking · Published June 13, 2026

Quick Answer

ITIN mortgages are offered by non-QM specialty lenders, credit unions (especially CDFIs), portfolio banks, and select independent mortgage brokers, not by large national banks like Chase or Wells Fargo. Rates typically run 0.5%-2% above conventional loans, and most programs require a 10%-20% down payment.

If you hold an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number and want to buy a home, your biggest challenge is not your income, your tax history, or your intent, it’s knowing where to look. Most mainstream banks won’t help, but a real market of lenders actively underwrites ITIN home loans. This guide breaks down each lender type, what to expect, and how to compare your options so you don’t overpay.

Why can’t I just apply at my regular bank?

A question we hear often:

Most large national banks, think Chase, Bank of America, or Wells Fargo, simply do not offer ITIN mortgage programs. Large national banks generally do not offer ITIN mortgage programs. The reason comes down to structure: ITIN mortgage loans are classified as non-QM (non-Qualified Mortgage) loans because they do not meet the lending criteria set by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, and as a result, they are usually held in lender portfolios or sold to private investors rather than government-sponsored enterprises.

That’s not a reflection of borrower quality, it’s a secondary-market technicality. This classification does not reflect borrower quality; rather, it reflects how the loan can be structured and sold. The practical result is higher down payment requirements and higher interest rates, which compensate lenders for the added risk of holding or privately selling the loan.

The good news: ITIN mortgages are most commonly offered by non-QM specialty lenders, credit unions serving immigrant communities, portfolio banks, and mortgage brokers with access to wholesale lenders. Each of these channels has different strengths, which we cover below.

What are the four main types of ITIN mortgage lenders?

Understanding the landscape before you start shopping will save you weeks of frustration.

1. Non-QM Specialty Lenders

While traditional banks often issue automatic rejections for non-SSN applicants, specialized ITIN mortgage lenders are filling the gap as the non-QM market expands. Non-QM lenders set their own underwriting rules and evaluate your full financial picture rather than running a government checklist. ITIN lenders use a manual underwriting process, instead of just pulling a FICO score from a computer, a human underwriter looks at your full financial picture to assess risk. These lenders are typically available nationwide and offer the widest range of loan amounts and terms.

2. Credit Unions (Especially CDFIs)

Very few large lenders offer ITIN loans, as they are niche products. Credit unions and CDFIs (Community Development Financial Institutions) tend to be the primary lenders in this market. CDFIs hold a special certification from the U.S. Treasury that grants them regulatory flexibility. CDFIs are not subject to ability-to-repay/qualified mortgage rule requirements, and small banks and credit unions with under $10 billion in assets where the loans are held in portfolio are subject to less restrictive rules under the Dodd-Frank Act, giving lenders more flexibility on the rates and terms they can offer. The main limitation with credit unions is membership: borrowers may need to live, work, or have an affiliation within a specific geographic area or organization to qualify for membership.

3. Portfolio Banks

Portfolio banks keep loans on their own balance sheets instead of selling them to the secondary market, which gives them the freedom to underwrite non-standard borrowers. A handful of regional banks, such as Old National Bank, which has a special home loan program for people in the Chicago area and Milwaukee who hold Individual Taxpayer Identification Numbers, have built dedicated ITIN programs for the communities they serve. Availability is usually regional, so check for community banks with a strong immigrant-borrower presence in your state.

4. Independent Mortgage Brokers

Several independent mortgage banks also offer ITIN loans. Independent mortgage banks do not have portfolios, so they sell to credit unions, banks, or mortgage real estate investment trusts. A good independent broker can submit your file to multiple wholesale ITIN lenders simultaneously, effectively shopping the market for you. This is often the fastest path if you are in a state with limited local options.

What interest rates and down payments should I expect?

This one comes up a lot:

ITIN mortgage rates are usually 0.5% to 2% higher than conventional loan rates, and down payment requirements are higher, typically 10% to 20%. These differences reflect the non-QM structure and limited secondary market options. Some programs and sources put the rate premium as high as 1%-3% depending on credit and loan size, interest rates for these products can range from 6.87% to 8.50% in the current rate environment.

On the down payment side, the range is wider than many borrowers realize:

Down PaymentWho It Applies To
10% minimumBorrowers with excellent credit (620-660+ FICO), strong income docs
15%-20% (most common)Standard ITIN non-QM and portfolio bank programs
20%-25%Some lenders, or borrowers with limited credit history

A larger down payment can improve your approval chances and reduce your interest rate. If you can push from 10% to 20%, you’ll often qualify for better pricing and fewer documentation hurdles. You may also use a gift from a relative to help with the down payment, for example, if you need $20,000 down, a relative can provide a portion of that amount.

What documents do all ITIN lenders basically require?

The main requirements for ITIN holders to buy a home include a valid ITIN, two years of tax returns filed with that number, proof of steady income (like pay stubs or employer letters), recent bank statements, and a government-issued photo ID.

For credit, lenders split into two camps:

  • Traditional credit: Most programs set a minimum score around 620. Most ITIN mortgage programs require a minimum credit score of around 620. Some lenders accept alternative credit documentation for borrowers without traditional credit histories, and higher credit scores typically qualify for lower down payments and better ITIN mortgage rates.
  • Alternative credit: If you have no U.S. credit score, many ITIN lenders accept alternative credit references in lieu of a traditional credit score, this includes verified payment history for rent, utilities, car insurance, phone bills, and other recurring obligations, with typically 3 to 4 references with at least 12 months of on-time payment history required.

For income, self-employed borrowers have a distinct path. Bank statement programs are commonly used by self-employed ITIN borrowers whose tax returns do not reflect their actual cash flow, since business owners often deduct legitimate expenses that reduce taxable income but do not reduce the money available to make mortgage payments. Under these programs, lenders review 12 to 24 months of bank deposits to calculate qualifying income, with personal bank statements usually applying a standard expense factor of around 50%, meaning half of the average monthly deposits are treated as usable income.

Finally, most lenders also cap your debt-to-income ratio. The debt-to-income ratio measures total monthly debt obligations relative to gross monthly income, and most ITIN programs cap DTI between 45% and 50%.

Are ITIN mortgages actually legal? I’ve heard conflicting things.

From a legal standpoint, no federal law requires a Social Security number to obtain a mortgage. Federal regulations from the CFPB make clear that immigrants should not be excluded from credit solely for lacking a Social Security number if they are otherwise eligible, but actual day-to-day policies tend to be slower to change, especially among large banks.

The scale of the market confirms its legitimacy. According to the IRS Taxpayer Advocate Service, there were 5.4 million active ITINs as of January 2021, and since the inception of the ITIN program, the IRS has issued about 26 million ITINs through the end of 2022. A meaningful share of those holders are homeowners or aspiring homeowners, and ITIN holders pay billions of dollars in federal taxes each year, and ITIN mortgage programs recognize these contributions and provide a path to homeownership for tax-paying residents without a Social Security number.

How do I compare ITIN mortgage lenders without wasting weeks?

Readers frequently ask:

The most important thing to understand is that ITIN mortgage terms are not standardized. Many borrowers who qualify in practice still struggle to secure an ITIN mortgage because the process breaks down before key details are reviewed, rates, lender requirements, and documentation standards vary widely, and those differences often do not surface until after an application is already underway.

Here’s how to compare efficiently:

  1. Get pre-qualified with at least two or three lenders before committing. Because ITIN rates vary significantly, shopping around is not just optional, it’s a financial necessity.
  2. Compare the APR, not just the rate. Don’t just look at the interest rate; compare the Annual Percentage Rate (APR), which gives you the true cost including all fees, because origination fees and closing costs can add up fast.
  3. Ask specifically about alternative credit acceptance. Not every ITIN lender accepts the same documentation for borrowers without a U.S. credit score. Confirm upfront whether they’ll count rent, utility bills, or international credit.
  4. Confirm property type eligibility. ITIN loans can be utilized for purchasing both primary residences and investment properties, but not every lender offers both. Confirm this early.
  5. Ask about loan limits. Depending on your financial situation, some programs allow loan amounts up to $1.25 million, which covers most purchase prices. Confirm the ceiling for any program you’re considering.

What’s the difference between an ITIN mortgage and a foreign national mortgage?

These two products are often confused but serve different borrowers.

Who it’s forITIN holders living and earning in the U.S.Non-residents living abroad
Income sourceU.S.-based, documented via tax returns or bank statementsForeign income or rental income (DSCR)
Down payment10%-20% typically25%-40% typically
Credit requirementU.S. or alternative creditStrong asset documentation
Rate premium~0.5%-2% above conventional~1.5%-3% above conventional

Guidance on acceptable ITIN lending is shaped by Qualified Mortgage rules and the Equal Credit Opportunity Act. Lenders are prohibited from discriminating based on national origin or immigration status if all income is verifiable and legal. Both loan types are legal; the right one depends entirely on where you live and where your income comes from.

FAQs

Can I get an ITIN mortgage from a big bank like Chase or Wells Fargo? Generally, no. Large national banks do not offer ITIN mortgage programs. You’ll need to work with non-QM specialty lenders, credit unions, CDFIs, or portfolio banks that underwrite ITIN loans on their own books.

What credit score do I need for an ITIN mortgage? Most ITIN mortgage lenders require a minimum score around 620-660. If you have no U.S. credit score, many lenders will accept alternative credit documentation such as 12 months of on-time rent, utility, or phone bill payments.

How much higher are interest rates on ITIN mortgages compared to conventional loans? ITIN mortgage rates typically run 0.5% to 2% higher than conventional loan rates, though some lenders cite a range of up to 3% depending on your credit profile, down payment, and loan amount. Shopping multiple lenders is essential.

Do ITIN mortgage lenders require two years of tax returns? Most do. Standard ITIN mortgage guidelines call for two years of U.S. tax returns filed with your ITIN, plus two years of verifiable employment history. Self-employed borrowers can often substitute 12-24 months of bank statements instead.

Is an ITIN mortgage a legal loan product in the U.S.? Yes. There is no federal law requiring a Social Security number to obtain a mortgage. Federal regulations from the CFPB make clear that immigrants should not be excluded from credit solely for lacking an SSN if they are otherwise eligible.

What is the minimum down payment for an ITIN mortgage? The minimum is typically 10% with strong credit, but 15%-20% is the most common requirement. A few specialized programs advertise options as low as 3.5% in select states for highly qualified borrowers.

Common questions

Generally, no. Large national banks do not offer ITIN mortgage programs. You'll need to work with non-QM specialty lenders, credit unions, CDFIs, or portfolio banks that underwrite ITIN loans on their own books.

What is the minimum down payment for an ITIN mortgage?

Typically 10% with strong credit, though 15%-20% is the more common ask. A few specialized programs advertise as low as 3.5% in select states for highly qualified borrowers. Our guide to the FHA-style 3.5% down payment program for ITIN borrowers gets into how one of those actually works.

Yes. Down payment assistance, usually run by state and local housing finance agencies rather than the federal government, can supplement whatever down payment threshold your lender wants, 20% or 3.5%. Eligibility gets set locally, so ITIN acceptance varies and has to be confirmed directly with the program administrator.

Quick Answer

Yes, ITIN holders can get personal loans. Credit unions, community banks, and online lenders offer both secured and unsecured personal loans using an ITIN instead of an SSN. Approval and rates depend on your income and credit history. Secured loans are easier to get with thin credit, and on-time payments build your U.S. credit.

Can you get a personal loan with an ITIN?

Yes. A personal loan is a lump sum you repay in fixed monthly installments, and plenty of lenders approve them for ITIN holders. Credit unions are often the most welcoming, especially if you become a member first. Online and community lenders fill in the rest of the market.

Secured vs. unsecured options

If your credit history is thin, a secured personal loan, backed by a savings deposit, CD, or vehicle, is far easier to get and usually cheaper. As you build history, you can move to unsecured loans that need no collateral.

How much can you borrow?

Loan amounts range from a few hundred dollars to $50,000 or more, depending on income, credit, and the lender. If you're newer to U.S. credit, expect to start smaller and grow your borrowing power as you demonstrate on-time payments. If you need a smaller amount fast for an emergency, see our dedicated guide to ITIN cash loans.

Which type of lender should you start with?

Where you apply matters as much as your numbers. The ITIN holder's path usually runs through institutions that underwrite on income and documentation rather than leaning entirely on a credit score pulled by SSN. Credit unions are a common starting point: they are not-for-profit, member-owned cooperatives, and as of the National Credit Union Administration's data there are roughly 4,500 federally insured credit unions serving over 140 million members in the U.S. Many serve immigrant and Spanish-speaking communities directly and accept an ITIN to open membership. A simple decision framework

Run these checks in order. Each one narrows the field and protects your wallet before you commit:

  1. Confirm ITIN acceptance in writing. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau notes that lenders may accept an ITIN in place of an SSN, but it is a per-lender policy, not a rule. Ask directly and get it confirmed before you apply, a denial can still leave a hard-inquiry footprint.
  2. Match the loan to the need. Use a personal loan for a defined, one-time expense you can repay on a fixed schedule, debt consolidation, a medical bill, or a planned purchase. For ongoing or revolving needs, a card may fit better; see ITIN credit cards.
  3. Compare the APR, not the monthly payment. A lower monthly payment often hides a longer term and more total interest. The APR rolls rate plus fees into one number you can compare across lenders.
  4. Secured first if your file is thin. If you have little U.S. credit history, a secured or co-signed loan is both easier to get and cheaper, and on-time payments build the history that unlocks unsecured loans later.
  5. Read the fee schedule. Origination fees, prepayment penalties, and late fees vary by lender. Two loans at the same rate can cost very different amounts once fees are counted.

Why this matters: the CFPB's consumer complaint data consistently shows that surprise fees and terms borrowers didn't understand are among the most common loan complaints. Slowing down to compare APR and fees across two or three ITIN-friendly lenders is the single highest-leverage step you can take.

How a personal loan builds your credit

An installment loan that reports to the nationwide bureaus does double duty: it covers the expense and it builds your file. Payment history is the largest input into most credit scores, so a year of on-time personal-loan payments can meaningfully strengthen a thin ITIN credit profile, provided the lender reports to the bureaus. Confirm that it does before you sign; not every small lender reports. For the full picture of how ITIN files are built and matched, see our complete ITIN loans guide and ITIN vs SSN explainer.

Tips to get approved

  • Document steady income with pay stubs, tax returns, or bank statements.
  • Consider a credit union you can join, they're often the most ITIN-friendly.
  • Offer collateral or add a co-signer if your credit history is limited.
  • Compare several lenders; rates and approval criteria vary a lot.
  • Keep your ITIN current, an expired ITIN can stall verification; the IRS publishes which ITINs need renewal.

Common questions

Can I get a personal loan with an ITIN?

Yes. Credit unions, community banks, and online lenders offer personal loans to ITIN holders. You typically qualify on income and credit history rather than an SSN.

How much can I borrow with an ITIN personal loan?

Amounts vary widely, from a few hundred dollars up to $50,000 or more, based on your income, credit, and the lender. First-time borrowers with thin credit usually start smaller.

Do I need collateral?

Not always. Unsecured personal loans require no collateral but lean more on credit. Secured personal loans (backed by savings or a vehicle) are easier to get with limited history and often carry lower rates.

What can I use an ITIN personal loan for?

Most personal loans can be used for almost anything, debt consolidation, emergencies, medical bills, home repairs, or building credit. Check the lender for any restrictions.

Are rates high on ITIN personal loans?

Rates depend mostly on your credit profile. Borrowers with limited history pay more; a co-signer, collateral, or a credit union membership can bring rates down.

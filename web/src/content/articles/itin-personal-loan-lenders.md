You do not need a Social Security Number to borrow money in the United States.

No federal law prohibits lenders from issuing personal loans to borrowers without Social Security numbers. Lenders need a way to identify borrowers and verify tax reporting obligations, and an ITIN satisfies that requirement. The real challenge is knowing which lenders actually welcome ITIN holders and what they require. This guide covers the top lenders accepting ITINs in 2026, current rate ranges, the documents you need, and how to strengthen your application before you apply.

## Who actually offers personal loans to ITIN holders?

*A question we hear often:* most people assume their bank will say no before they even ask. That assumption is sometimes correct, but often not.

Approval is most common through credit unions, online lenders, and specialty finance companies. Many ITIN personal loans are unsecured, though secured options can come with lower rates. Major national banks are a tougher path: Chase, Bank of America, and Wells Fargo all require an SSN as standard policy. A few branches will accept ITINs for select products if you already have an existing relationship, but that is not something you can count on.

The best-documented ITIN-accepting personal loan sources in 2026:

- **Wells Fargo** (existing customers only): competitive APR range, large loan amounts, and repayment terms up to several years. You need a Wells Fargo account open for at least 12 months to be eligible.

- **Alliant Credit Union**: same-day funding is possible after approval. Loan amounts run $1,000-$100,000 with repayment terms of 12-60 months and a minimum APR of 8.74%.

- **Security Service FCU**: accepts ITIN holders with fair credit. Borrow $250-$50,000 over 3-66 months at 9.99%-17.95% APR.

- **PNC Bank**: lends $1,000-$35,000 with APRs starting at 8.14% depending on overall creditworthiness. A reasonable option for smaller loan amounts.

- **Star of Texas Credit Union (TX)**: explicitly lends to ITIN holders, charges no origination or prepayment fees, and focuses specifically on meeting members' needs including ITIN lending.

- **Resource One Credit Union (TX)**: treats an ITIN as valid identification and offers ITIN borrowers the same terms and conditions as SSN holders.

## What APR and loan amounts should I expect?

*This one comes up a lot:* borrowers are often surprised by the gap between advertised rates and what they are actually quoted.

Interest rates on ITIN personal loans run higher than comparable SSN-based loans. Typical APRs fall somewhere in the 12%-36% range, with some specialty lenders charging more. Credit strength drives the pricing. Borrowers with scores above 680 and stable income tend to land toward the low end; thin credit files push you toward the high end.

Without an SSN and with little or no U.S. credit history, expect 10%-36% APR. Credit unions sit at the low end of that range; fintech subprime lenders sit at the high end. If you have strong income, steady employment, and a U.S. credit file built over at least a year, the 10%-20% range is realistic.

On loan size: amounts typically run $500-$35,000. Borrowers with limited credit history or no prior installment loans usually start smaller, often $1,000-$5,000. Established credit, consistent income, and solid banking history can push that ceiling up depending on the lender.

## What documents do I need to apply?

Your ITIN letter proves the IRS assigned your tax identification number. The original CP565 works best, though CP567 (renewal confirmation) is also accepted. If you have lost your letter, request a replacement from the IRS. That process takes several weeks, so plan ahead.

Beyond the ITIN letter, most lenders ask for:

- Government-issued photo ID. Foreign passports are widely accepted, as are consular identification cards and some state-issued IDs that do not require an SSN.
- Proof of U.S. address: a utility bill, lease agreement, or bank statement with your name and address.
- Proof of employment and income, typically pay stubs or a job offer letter. Some lenders also require an active U.S. bank account before approving a loan.
- Two months of bank statements showing consistent deposits.

Incomplete or inconsistent documents are a common reason applications stall or get declined. Having everything ready before you start saves real time.

## How does the ITIN personal loan application process actually work?

The steps look similar to a standard personal loan, with two extra considerations: finding an ITIN-friendly lender first, and submitting a complete document package from the start.

1. **Confirm your ITIN is current.** The IRS expires ITINs that have not been used on a tax return in three consecutive years. If yours has lapsed, you must renew it via Form W-7 before any lender can process your application. Our [ITIN renewal guide](https://itinlending.net/itin-renewal) walks through how to do that quickly.

2. **Check your credit.** Even without an SSN, you may have a credit history if you have held previous financial accounts. Pull your reports from the major bureaus to understand where you stand. No credit history at all narrows your lender options but does not eliminate them.

3. **Pre-qualify with multiple lenders.** Online marketplaces let you see pre-qualified rates in minutes without a hard inquiry. Start there before committing to any formal application.

4. **Avoid applying everywhere at once.** Multiple hard inquiries within a short window will hurt your score. Be selective.

5. **Submit a complete file.** Gather your ITIN letter, ID, proof of income, bank statements, and proof of address before you start. Incomplete paperwork is the most preventable cause of denial. Missing documents give underwriters a reason to pause or decline a file that might otherwise pass.

## Are there red flags I should watch out for?

*A question we hear often:* ITIN borrowers are sometimes targeted by predatory lenders who charge sky-high rates or collect upfront fees before sending any money.

Financial experts generally treat 36% APR as the line between expensive and predatory. Any lender quoting you more than 36% on a personal loan deserves serious scrutiny. Other red flags: a lender that charges a fee before approving your loan, refuses to put the loan agreement in writing, or pressures you to borrow more than you asked for. Walk away from all of those.

The CFPB also makes clear that lenders cannot deny you based on national origin, race, or other protected characteristics. If you believe that has happened, you can file a complaint directly with the CFPB.

Stick to federally insured credit unions, CDFI-certified lenders, and well-reviewed fintechs. ## FAQs

**Can I get a personal loan with an ITIN and no credit history?**
Yes. Some lenders, including Capital Good Fund and certain credit unions, accept alternative credit references such as on-time rent and utility payments in place of a traditional credit score. Loan amounts are usually smaller, often $500-$3,000, until you build a U.S. credit file.

**What credit score do I need for an ITIN personal loan?**
Most ITIN-friendly lenders look for a score of 580-640 or above, though some specialty lenders have no minimum. Borrowers with scores above 680 typically qualify for the lowest APRs in the 10%-20% range.

**Do credit unions give personal loans to ITIN holders?**
Many do. Most lenders that offer ITIN loans are credit unions and community development financial institutions, according to the Urban Institute. Credit unions such as Security Service FCU, Alliant, Star of Texas CU, Red River CU, and Resource One CU explicitly accept ITINs and often provide lower rates than banks.

**What documents do I need to apply for an ITIN personal loan?**
You typically need your IRS ITIN letter (CP565 or CP567), a government-issued photo ID (passport or consular ID), proof of U.S. address, recent pay stubs or bank statements, and an active U.S. bank account.

**Is it legal for a lender to deny me just because I have an ITIN instead of an SSN?**
No federal law prohibits ITIN-based lending. Whether a lender accepts an ITIN is a business decision, not a legal limitation. Loans without an SSN are legal and widely available through lenders that choose to offer them. The CFPB also protects you from discrimination based on national origin.
